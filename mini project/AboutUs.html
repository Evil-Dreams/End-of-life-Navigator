About DNR

A do-not-resuscitate order, or DNR order, is a medical order written by a health care provider. It instructs providers not to do CPR (cardiopulmonary resuscitation) if a patient's breathing stops or if the patient's heart stops beating.

What is a DNR?

Ideally, a DNR order is created, or set up, before an emergency occurs. A DNR order allows you to choose whether or not you want CPR in an emergency. It is specific about CPR. It does not have instructions for other treatments, such as pain medicine, other medicines, or nutrition. The provider writes the order only after talking about it with the patient (if possible), the health care proxy, or the patient's family.

Procedure

The procedure for implementing a DNR order involves discussion between the patient, their family, and healthcare providers. It typically requires a signed document indicating the patient's wishes.

Eligibility

Eligibility for a DNR order varies depending on the patient's medical condition and their wishes. It is often considered for patients with terminal illnesses or those who do not wish to undergo CPR.

Benefits

The benefits of a DNR order include respecting the patient's autonomy and preventing unnecessary suffering. It allows patients to avoid invasive medical procedures that they do not desire.

Risks

While DNR orders can provide peace of mind for patients and families, there are potential risks, including the possibility of not receiving life-saving treatment in certain situations. It's essential to discuss these risks with healthcare providers.

Decision

When You are Unable to Make the Decision

Due to illness or injury, you may not be able to state your wishes about CPR. In this case:
  • If your provider has already written a DNR order at your request, your family may not override it.

  • You may have named someone to speak for you, such as a health care agent. If so, this person or a legal guardian can agree to a DNR order for you.
If you have not named someone to speak for you, under some circumstances, a family member can agree to a DNR order for you, but only when you are not able to make your own medical decisions.
